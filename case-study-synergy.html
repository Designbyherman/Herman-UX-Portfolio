Synergy Alliance
Problem

Community support existed, but finding the right resource was fragmented.

People often learn about community programs through flyers, social posts, referrals, websites, or word-of-mouth. That creates friction for anyone trying to quickly understand what help is available, who it is for, and how to take the next step.

Synergy Alliance was designed around a simple question: how might a mobile app make community resources easier to discover, compare, and act on?

Research Synthesis
Research

I used early research to understand what people needed from a community resource app.

The research focused on understanding the information people would need before engaging with a local organization or service. Instead of designing only around content categories, I looked at the decisions users would need to make: whether a program was relevant, whether it was available, and what action they could take next.

Research questions

  1. What types of local services or programs would people expect to find?
  2. What makes a community resource feel trustworthy?
  3. What information do people need before taking action?
  4. How should the app balance discovery, contact, events, and donations?

Early synthesis

  • Users needed resource information to be organized around real decisions, not just categories.
  • Program details needed to clarify who the resource was for, what it offered, and what the next step looked like.
  • The experience needed to support both browsing and direct action.
  • Trust depended on clarity, context, and visible pathways to contact or support.

Constraints

  • This was an early concept project, not a launched product.
  • Formal usability testing and post-launch analytics were outside the scope of this version.
  • Future validation would need to include community members, service providers, and people actively seeking support.
Synthesis

Research patterns were translated into three product requirements.

The early synthesis helped narrow the app from a broad community platform into a focused resource-discovery experience. The product needed to help people find relevant support, understand whether a resource applied to them, and move toward action with less uncertainty.

Discoverability

Resources needed to be easier to browse by need, program type, or community goal.

Context

Program details needed to explain who the resource was for, what it offered, and why it mattered.

Follow-through

Each screen needed clear next steps such as saving, contacting, registering, or donating.

Key Insight

The app did not just need to list resources. It needed to help people understand what each resource offered, whether it applied to them, and what action they could take next.

Concept Proposal

A mobile-first community resource hub that helps people discover programs, learn what they offer, and take action without having to search across disconnected channels.

Outcomes / Reflection

This project helped me understand how service access depends on information architecture, not just visual design.

Community products need clear pathways

A resource app has to do more than display information. It needs to help people understand what is available, why it matters, and what action to take.

Early concepts still need honest scope

Synergy Alliance was an early design concept, not a launched product. The next step would be validating the flow with community members, service providers, and people actively seeking support.
